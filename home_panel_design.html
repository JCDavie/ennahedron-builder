Enneahedron home — insulated panel system & cost sheet

Turning the 9 enneahedron faces (3 squares + 6 kites, L = 6 m) into a habitable shell. Companion to mitre_drawing.html and cut_plan_6m.html.

Read me first — assumptions in this document

  • No prior cost sheets exist in the repo. Every price below is a 2026 indicative figure for a temperate-climate self-build, in NZD/AUD-equivalent ranges. Edit the UNIT_RATES object in the script to fit your supplier quotes.
  • The wall stack is a structural timber-cassette panel (not foam-core SIP) — easier to fabricate at 6 m edges, repairable, and compatible with the mitre cuts already drawn.
  • One enneahedron module = one habitable cell. The bottom square (F5) is the floor; the two upright squares (F0, F8) host the door and a feature window; the 6 kites form the canted roof/walls.
  • All bevel angles from the mitre drawing are unchanged — they apply to the structural core of the panel. Inner and outer skins are applied after the core is cut.

Edge framing — clean bevels without splintering

OSB and I-joist webs tear when run across a bevel cut, and the chipped edge prevents two panels seating tightly. The fix is to laminate a continuous strip of solid pine (or knot-free Douglas fir / LVL) along the perimeter of the cassette core before bevelling. The saw cuts only this clean timber, leaving a sharp, splinter-free, paintable edge.

SpecValue
Edge laminate stockSolid radiata pine, FJ-clear grade, KD 12% MC. (Equivalent: knot-free Douglas fir, or LVL when budget allows.)
Section90 × 90 mm (wall) / 90 × 120 mm (kite roof) — full thickness of the I-joist + 5 mm proud either side, planed flush after gluing
Length per panelSquare: 4 × 6.0 m = 24 m. Kite: 2 × 6.0 m + 2 × 2.83 m = 17.66 m
Total pine for 9 panels3 × 24 + 6 × 17.66 = 178 m linear (≈ 1.6 m³ of dressed pine)
Adhesive1-part PUR (Purbond / Titebond Polyurethane) — gap-filling, structural, weather-tolerant
Cut order1) Build I-joist cassette to inner-skin shape  2) Mitre the pine strips at the corner angles (use the kite interior angles 41.4° / 110.7° / 97.2° / 110.7°)  3) Glue + clamp pine to all four edges  4) Plane flush  5) Run the bevel cut through the pine on the mitre saw
Why pine specificallyClosed, even grain → cuts cleanly across grain at the 33.90° / 40.89° bevel without fibre tear-out. Soft enough to compress slightly under the EPDM gasket for an airtight seal. Holds threaded inserts well. Paint- or oil-finishable.
Edge cost allowance178 m × $14/m dressed pine ≈ $2,500 + adhesive $400 + labour 8 h ≈ $3,500 total

Demountable bolt-together connection

The connection has to be structurally tight for life-safety and weatherproofing, but fully reversible so the home can be unbolted, flat-packed, and re-erected on a different site. The detail below uses no glue or sealant on the structural fasteners — only on the EPDM gasket (which is replaceable).

ComponentSpec
Through-boltM16 × 220 mm grade 8.8 hot-dip galvanised, hex head, with hardened washer + nyloc nut
Threaded insertM16 brass insert (E-Z LOK 400-016) epoxied into pre-drilled 28 mm hole in the pine edge laminate
Spacing600 mm centres on every edge (every 4th I-joist bay), staggered between adjacent panels so bolts don't clash
Bolt count per home(12 × L-edges × ⌈6000/600⌉) + (6 × S-edges × ⌈2829/600⌉) = 120 + 30 = 150 bolts
GasketEPDM closed-cell, 25 × 12 mm bulb section, continuous around every edge — compresses to ~6 mm at design clamping force
Tightening torque110 Nm (≈80 ft·lb) — gives ~30 kN clamp per bolt without crushing the pine
ToolsOne ½″ drive impact wrench + 24 mm socket. Assemble or disassemble in ~3 hours per panel pair (2-person crew).
Cover stripRemovable 60 × 6 mm aluminium "Z-flashing" snaps over each external seam, hiding bolt heads and shedding rain. Held by self-drilling screws into the pine — also removable.

Move-and-rebuild procedure (whole home)

  1. Strip removable items. Inner plasterboard cover strips (clip-out), Z-flashings outside (unscrew), interior trim.
  2. Disconnect services. Plumbing, electrical, gas at module-edge unions designed for it (left as future work).
  3. Disassembly order — top down. Roof kites first (F2, F3, F6, F7), then upright squares (F0, F8) and remaining lower kites (F1, F4), then floor (F5) last. Crane required for square panels (~280 kg each); kites (~190 kg) can be carried by 4 people.
  4. Inspect & replace gaskets. EPDM has a 10-year service life under UV — assume one full re-gasket per relocation.
  5. Transport. All 9 panels stack flat to about 1.0 m total on a 7 × 4 m flatbed trailer (kites within square footprint). Pine edges face inward to protect them.
  6. Re-erection. Reverse order. Same 6-day crane schedule listed in the cost sheet. Allow a fresh blower-door test at the new site.

Notes for the build

  • Sequence. Cut all 9 cassette cores (timber I-joist frame in plan-view shape) on the mitre saw → fit OSB skins → fit insulation → close inner skin → assemble into shell on site → apply external cladding last (over the joints).
  • Joints. Butt the cores along the bevels per the mitre drawing. Seal continuously with a vapour-tight tape on the inside skin, and a UV-stable EPDM gasket compressed between cladding battens on the outside.
  • Structural sizing. 240 mm I-joists @ 600 mm centres are adequate for the wall faces; 300 mm @ 400 mm centres for the kite roof faces given snow + access loads. Validate with a structural engineer for your jurisdiction; the canted geometry transfers most lateral load through the rim diagonal of each kite.
  • Glazing the F8 square. A single 6 × 6 m IGU is impractical. Split into a 2 × 3 grid of 2.0 × 3.0 m triple-glazed panes carried by a steel cruciform mullion. The mullion sits on the inside face; the bevels at the rim are unchanged.
  • Fire/escape. The F0 entry door is one egress; add a roof-light hatch in F2 or F3 (kite face) as second egress per most residential codes.
  • Bevel direction (critical). Outer skin is the LONG side of the bevel; inner skin is the SHORT side. Mark the OUTSIDE face on every cassette before cutting.
