TS 75 tolerance analysis — can you build the enneahedron with it?

Translates the Festool TS 75's mechanical accuracy into millimetres of joint misfit on the enneahedron, and answers whether the panels will close. Companion to mitre_drawing.html, home_panel_design.html, and sa_suppliers.html.

Verdict

Yes — the TS 75 + a Wixey WR300 angle gauge (~R 1,500) easily hits ±0.2° per cut, which is 5× tighter than the 1° threshold you asked about and well inside the gasket budget of the demountable panel system.

Below: how the angle error translates to millimetres of joint gap, how it compounds across the 18-edge solid, your tolerance budget, and a practical workflow to reliably hit ±0.2°.

How angle error translates to mm of joint gap

For a panel of structural-core thickness t, an angular error of Δθ on the bevel opens a gap at the back of the joint of:

gap_at_back = t × tan(Δθ)

At the recommended core thickness t = 240 mm

How error compounds across the 18-edge solid

Around any single vertex, 3 or 4 panels meet. If their bevels are all off by Δθ in the same direction (worst case), the panels can't all reach the vertex. Sweep at the far end of an L = 6 m edge:

vertex_gap_worstcase ≈ L × Δθ_radians = 6000 mm × Δθ° × 0.01745
Δθ per cut Worst-case gap at far end of 6 m edge Realistic last-panel forced fit (random walk) Result
±0.1° 10 mm 5 – 10 mm Bolts pull silently. Factory-precise look.
±0.2° 21 mm 10 – 20 mm Closes with audible "thunk" — fine.
±0.5° 52 mm 25 – 50 mm You'll notice. May need to slot one bolt hole.
±1.0° 105 mm 50 – 100 mm Don't try this. Re-cut.
The "realistic" column accounts for the fact that random errors partially cancel — 18 edges in an enneahedron, with errors of standard deviation σ, give a closure misfit of order L × σ_rad rather than L × N × σ_rad. The polyhedron is a closed solid, so the last panel forces the others into compliance: that closure stress is what the gasket and bolts absorb.

Practical workflow to reliably hit ±0.2° on the TS 75

  1. Stick the Wixey to the body of the saw blade (not the tilt scale). Press zero with the saw flat at 0° on a known-flat reference.
  2. Tilt to 33.90° (for L-edges) or 40.89° (for S-edges) reading the Wixey, not the saw's analogue scale. Lock the tilt knob hard.
  3. Cut a 200 mm test offcut from scrap pine of the same thickness as your panel core.
  4. Stand two offcuts from the same cut face-to-face. Place the Wixey across the back. It should read 2 × bevel:
    • L-cut: target 67.79°
    • S-cut: target 81.79°
  5. Adjust the saw tilt by half the error and re-cut. Two iterations is enough to land under 0.1°.
  6. Once dialled in, mark the saw position with a pencil line and don't touch it for the rest of that bevel-angle's panels. Cut all 12 L-edges in one session, then all 6 S-edges in another — never mid-stream switch.
  7. Recheck the Wixey on the blade every 20 minutes — sawdust under the magnet will shift the reading by 0.1–0.2°.

Length tolerance (the other axis)

Cutting straight along the rail with the TS 75 is good to ±0.2 mm over 3 m in normal hands — better than any framing tolerance you'll need. Two strategies for the 6 m square edges:

  • One rail, indexed: Make a hard stop at the end of the first cut, slide the rail, butt it against the stop, finish the cut. Holds ±0.5 mm over 6 m.
  • Two rails + connector: The Festool FSV connector joins two FS 3000/2 rails to give a 6 m guide. Holds ±0.3 mm over 6 m if installed carefully on a flat reference.

Common mistakes that blow your tolerance budget

  • Trusting the saw's analogue scale — it's a 0.5° readout at best, and the pointer parallax adds another 0.2°. Always set the angle with the Wixey.
  • Cutting half your L-bevels on Monday and half on Friday — heat, dust and re-tightening the tilt knob will drift the angle by 0.2° between sessions. Cut all of one angle in one go.
  • Leaving the splinter strip un-aligned — first cut on a fresh rail trims the strip; the cut line then reads precisely there. Don't try to set up after the strip is off-line.
  • Ignoring blade flex — a dull or thin-kerf blade in 240 mm-deep stock will deflect 0.5–1.0 mm. Use Festool's HW Universal blade and feed slowly.
  • Cutting bevels through OSB instead of solid pine edge — chip-out forces you to over-cut to clean up, and the joint loses the gasket compression line. Always glue the perimeter pine laminate before bevelling (see home_panel_design.html).

Bottom line

The enneahedron panel system was designed with a ±5 mm per-joint tolerance budget. The TS 75 + Wixey delivers 0.84 mm per joint at ±0.2°. That's a 6× safety margin on a one-off owner-builder kit costing about R 25,000.

Spend the extra R 250 k – R 1.5 M on industrial machinery only if you're going to build many homes and want the speed and dial-in-once repeatability. For one home, the TS 75 + Wixey + the test-cut workflow above is the right tool.

The single most important purchase for accuracy is not the saw — it's the Wixey WR300 at R 1,500.
